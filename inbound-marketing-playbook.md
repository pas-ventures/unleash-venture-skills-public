# Inbound Marketing Playbook

> Combined skill: Landing Page Builder + Drip Campaign Builder
> Two connected assets that work together as a conversion funnel.

## When to Use
- "Build a landing page and drip campaign for [target group]"
- "Create an inbound funnel for [product/service]"
- "Write a nurture sequence for [audience]"
- "Build a lead capture page for [niche]"

## Overview

This playbook builds two connected conversion assets:

1. **Landing Page** — Captures the email address (pain-driven, research-backed, exit popup)
2. **Drip Campaign** — 12 emails over 25 days that nurture the lead to a booking/consultation

Both are built from the same research foundation: real problems found on the web, in YouTube transcripts, forums, and case studies where the target group speaks about their own struggles.

## Core Frameworks

### Dan Kennedy — The Ultimate Sales Letter
- Enter the conversation already happening in their head
- Agitate the pain until it's unbearable
- Discredit the alternatives they've already tried
- Present the solution as inevitable
- Stack value until saying "no" feels irrational
- Close with urgency and future pacing

### Alex Hormozi — $100M Offers + $100M Leads
- **Value Equation:** Dream Outcome x Perceived Likelihood / Time Delay x Effort & Sacrifice
- **Grand Slam Offer:** Stack components, show total value vs. price
- **Risk Reversal:** Guarantee that eliminates buyer risk (e.g., 90-day pilot, money back)
- **Opportunity Cost:** Make the cost of inaction concrete and painful
- **Scarcity:** Capacity limits, pilot spots, personal onboarding constraints

### Russell Brunson — DotCom Secrets + Expert Secrets
- **Epiphany Bridge:** Tell a story that leads to a breakthrough moment
- **Hook-Story-Offer:** Every touchpoint hooks attention, tells a story, makes an offer
- **Secret Formula:** Who (dream customer) → Where (where they congregate) → Bait (what value to offer) → Result (what outcome)
- **Stack Slide:** Layer bonuses and value components until the offer is irresistible
- **False Beliefs:** Identify and break the 3 core false beliefs (vehicle, internal, external)

## PHASE 0: Research Foundation

> This phase feeds BOTH the landing page and the drip campaign. Run it once, use it everywhere.

### Step 0.1: Define the Target Group
Get from the user:
- **Product/Service:** What are we selling?
- **Primary target group:** (e.g., NGOs, property managers, trades businesses)
- **Geography:** (e.g., DACH, Germany-only, global)
- **Language:** (e.g., German — Sie/Du, English)
- **Company size:** (solo, 2-10, 10-50, 50+)
- **Decision maker role:** (Geschaeftsfuehrer, Inhaber, Vorstand, etc.)

### Step 0.2: Split into Sub-Target Groups
Break the primary target group into 3-5 sub-segments with different daily realities and pains.

Example for "NGOs":
- Small volunteer-run associations (<5 staff)
- Mid-size NGOs with paid staff (5-20)
- Large established nonprofits (20+)
- Newly founded initiatives
- Sector-specific: social, environmental, cultural, humanitarian

Confirm with the user which sub-segments to focus on, or research all.

### Step 0.3: Deep Web Research

This is the core differentiator. Use web search and web fetch extensively.

#### YouTube Transcript Mining
Search for videos where the target group talks about their own problems:
- `"{target group}" Probleme site:youtube.com`
- `"{target group}" Herausforderungen Interview`
- `"{target group}" Alltag Erfahrungsbericht`
- `"{target group}" frustriert` / `"{target group}" struggles`
- `"{target group}" day in the life`
- Conference talks, panel discussions, podcasts featuring the target group

**What to extract:**
- Direct quotes about daily frustrations
- Specific numbers they mention (hours wasted, money lost, % of time on admin)
- Emotional language they use to describe their problems
- Stories about failed solutions they've tried
- What they wish existed

#### Forum & Community Mining
- Reddit threads, industry forums, Facebook groups
- `"{target group}" biggest challenge site:reddit.com`
- Industry association publications (Verbaende, Innungen, Kammern)
- Quora, LinkedIn discussions
- Trade press / Fachpresse

#### Case Studies & Reports
- Industry studies with hard data (%, EUR, hours)
- Government reports / Statistisches Bundesamt
- Consulting firm reports (McKinsey, Deloitte sector reports)
- Academic research on the sector's operational challenges

#### News & Trend Articles
- Recent news about regulatory changes affecting the target group
- Industry trend pieces showing growing pressure
- Competitor analysis: what solutions exist and why they fall short

### Step 0.4: Compile Research Output
Produce a research document with:
- **Top 5 pains** ranked by severity (with sources + hard numbers)
- **5-10 direct quotes** from real people in the target group
- **Hard numbers** (hours, euros, percentages) for each pain
- **Failed alternatives** the target group has already tried
- **Emotional core:** Why they started, what frustrates them, what they dream of
- **Industry jargon:** The exact terms and language they use daily
- **3 False Beliefs** (Brunson): vehicle, internal, external

### Step 0.5: Gather Niche Intel Summary
Before building anything, confirm you have:
- [ ] Niche name and market size (# of businesses/organizations)
- [ ] Decision maker role
- [ ] Top 3-5 pains with specific numbers (hours lost, money left on table, % of time wasted)
- [ ] Formality level (Du or Sie)
- [ ] Industry-specific jargon
- [ ] Product/service name + differentiator
- [ ] At least 3 direct quotes from the target group
- [ ] 3 False Beliefs identified

Use the **Niche Research skill** if this data isn't complete.

## PHASE 1: Landing Page

### Step 1.1: Build the Hook (Hero Section)
- **Urgency badge:** Lead with the most shocking stat from research (e.g., "73% nennen X als #1 Problem")
- **Headline:** State the pain in concrete numbers, then promise: "So loesen es die Besten der Branche"
- **Sub-headline:** Name the top 2-3 pains explicitly. Emphasize: no IT project, no technical knowledge, from the industry
- **CTA:** Curiosity-driven, NOT product-driven. Pattern: "Jetzt herausfinden, wie [target group] unter [size] [pain] loesen"
- **Trust line:** "Kein IT-Projekt · Kein technisches Wissen noetig · Von [target group] fuer [target group] · Server in Deutschland"

#### CTA Principles
- **Drive curiosity, don't pitch product:** "Jetzt herausfinden wie..." not "Kostenlos testen"
- **No AI language:** Say "Automatisierung", "Entlastung", "Assistent" — not "KI" or "AI"
- **Frame as secret knowledge:** "How the best ones do it" — makes people want to peek behind the curtain
- **Low commitment:** "herausfinden", "ansehen", "erfahren" — not "anfragen", "buchen", "kaufen"

### Step 1.2: Pain Story Section
- Write a narrative "Monday morning" story using the target group's real daily pain
- Include specific scenarios (names, places, situations from research)
- End with a pullquote that captures the emotional core
- Transition: "Trotzdem gibt es [target group] die es anders machen..."

### Step 1.3: Stats Bar
- 4 data points from the research, with sources cited
- Use the most shocking/relatable numbers
- Format: Big number + short explanation

### Step 1.4: Feature Cards (Pain-Point Driven)
- 6 cards, each named after a PAIN being solved (not a feature)
- Pattern: "[Pain] baendigen/beenden/im Griff" — not "AI Email Triage"
- Each card: Pain description + Before/After comparison
- Use real industry terminology the target group uses daily

### Step 1.5: How It Works
- 4 simple steps: Check machen → Gespraech → Verbinden → Laeuft
- Emphasize: No IT, no technical knowledge, no team training needed
- Position the team as industry peers, not tech people

### Step 1.6: Daily Example / Product Mock
- Show a realistic interaction scenario
- Use target-group-specific situations from research
- Make it feel like "someone already did my morning work"

### Step 1.7: Case Study ("Stell dir vor")
- Aspirational narrative: What a good day looks like WITH the product
- End with 4 result boxes (time saved, tasks automated, no system changes, your pace)

### Step 1.8: Testimonials
- 3 testimonials: 2 from the target group, 1 cross-industry credibility
- Frame as operational relief, NOT technology praise
- Use real testimonials when available; mark placeholders clearly

### Step 1.9: Objections (FAQ)
- 5-6 objections, reframed from target group perspective
- Lead with "Ich bin kein Technik-Mensch" — always the #1 fear
- Address: time to implement, data privacy, false actions, existing software compatibility

### Step 1.10: Comparison Table
- Side-by-side: "Klassische Loesung" vs "[Product]"
- Emphasize: No migration, no training, no team involvement

### Step 1.11: Guarantee
- Pilot period, zero risk
- Limited spots creates urgency

### Step 1.12: Team Section
- Frame as industry people, NOT tech people
- "Started because they experienced the same problem"

### Step 1.13: Exit-Intent Popup
- "5 Fehler, die [target group] am meisten kosten"
- Pain-focused mistakes (waiting for staff, big software projects, thinking automation is for big firms)
- CTA: Same curiosity-driven pattern

### Step 1.14: Build Signup Page (Progressive Capture)

**Flow — email is ALWAYS captured first:**

| Step | Fields | Required? | Skip? |
|------|--------|-----------|-------|
| 1 | Email + Name | Email only | No |
| 2 | Phone | No | Yes |
| 3 | Position + Team size | No | Yes |
| 4 | Quiz: Org size | Click = advance | Auto |
| 5 | Quiz: Biggest time waster | Click = advance | Auto |
| 6 | Quiz: What held you back | Multi-select | Submit |

**Key rules:**
- Email fires to backend IMMEDIATELY at step 1
- `beforeunload` saves whatever data collected if they close tab
- No required fields except email — nothing blocks the flow
- Enter key works on every step
- Success screen shows booking link

### Landing Page Technical Notes
- Single HTML files, no build tools
- Google Fonts: DM Sans + Caveat (or adjust per brand)
- CSS variables for theming (swap colors per brand)
- Scroll animations via IntersectionObserver
- Exit-intent via mouseout on document
- Form data logged to console with TODO for webhook endpoint

### Landing Page File Naming
- `{target-group-slug}.html` — Landing page
- `{target-group-slug}-signup.html` — Signup flow

## PHASE 2: Drip Campaign (12 Emails, 25 Days)

> Triggered when a lead submits their email on the landing page from Phase 1.

### Step 2.1: Identify the 3 False Beliefs (Russell Brunson)
From the research, identify:
1. **Vehicle false belief:** "This type of solution won't work for us" → Break with proof
2. **Internal false belief:** "I/we can't do this" → Break with ease of use / onboarding
3. **External false belief:** "External factors prevent this" → Break with data privacy, regulation compliance, etc.

### Step 2.2: Define the Consistent CTA
One CTA used in EVERY email. Pattern:
> "Erfahre jetzt von einem Experten, wie andere [target group] dieses Problem heute bereits loesen."

Adjust wording to fit the product and audience, but keep it consistent across all 12 emails.

### Step 2.3: Write the 12-Email Sequence

#### Sequence Architecture

| # | Day | Purpose | Framework | Email Archetype |
|---|-----|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| 1 | 0 | Welcome + pain confirmation | DK: Enter the conversation | "You took the first step — here's why it matters" |
| 2 | 1 | Name the real problem | DK: Agitate; Hormozi: Dream Outcome | "Why [common workaround] is destroying your [business/mission]" |
| 3 | 3 | Discredit alternatives | DK: Discredit; Brunson: Vehicle false belief | "3 solutions everyone tries — and why none work" |
| 4 | 5 | Reveal the solution (no selling) | DK: Present Solution; Hormozi: Dream | "What if [dream morning/day] looked like this?" |
| 5 | 7 | Social proof + results | Hormozi: Proof Stack; DK: Testimonials | "[Specific result quote from customer/peer]" |
| 6 | 9 | Cost of inaction | DK: Cost of Inaction; Hormozi: Opportunity Cost | "What your current situation really costs (here's the math)" |
| 7 | 11 | Fear trigger + solution | DK: Fear of Loss; Brunson: Epiphany Bridge | "[Specific risk]. [Amount]. Personal liability." |
| 8 | 13 | Deep-dive on #1 pain | Hormozi: Remove Obstacles; DK: Specificity | "[Vivid scenario of their worst daily pain]" |
| 9 | 16 | Deep-dive on #2 pain | DK: Stack Value; Hormozi: Time to Value | "[Time-saving calculation with concrete numbers]" |
| 10 | 19 | Objection handling | DK: Overcome Objections; Brunson: External false belief | "Your biggest concern — the honest answer" |
| 11 | 22 | The offer (value stack) | Hormozi: Grand Slam Offer; Brunson: Stack Slide | "[Price] vs. [cost of status quo]. Your choice." |
| 12 | 25 | Last chance + future pacing | DK: Urgency; Brunson: Epiphany Bridge; Hormozi: FOMO | "In 12 months you'll look back — and wish you'd started today" |

#### Email-by-Email Writing Guide

**Email 1 — Welcome & Pain Confirmation** (Day 0, immediate)
- Framework: DK — "Enter the conversation already happening in their head"
- Open by acknowledging their action (leaving their email)
- Paint a vivid "Monday morning" scenario using research quotes and real situations
- Name the structural problem (it's not about working harder)
- Promise: "In the next days I'll show you what the best [target group] do differently"
- Tone: Empathetic peer, zero selling

**Email 2 — The Real Problem** (Day 1)
- Framework: DK — Agitate the Pain; Hormozi — Dream Outcome vs. Likelihood
- Hard numbers calculation (hours/week on admin, cost per year, hidden costs)
- Name the paradox: "You started this to [motivation]. Now you're trapped in [admin hell]"
- Industry-level stat showing it's systemic, not personal
- Tease: "Tomorrow I'll show you why the 3 obvious solutions all fail"

**Email 3 — Frustrating Alternatives** (Day 3)
- Framework: DK — Discredit the Alternatives; Brunson — Break vehicle false belief
- Three alternatives the target group typically tries:
  1. Hire more staff (expensive, hard to find, slow to onboard)
  2. Buy industry software (complex, no one uses it properly, doesn't solve the real problem)
  3. Just work harder (burnout, errors, family suffers)
- Why none solve the structural problem
- Tease: "There's a fourth option. I'll tell you tomorrow."

**Email 4 — Reveal the Solution (No Selling)** (Day 5)
- Framework: DK — Present the Solution; Hormozi — Dream Outcome
- "Imagine this:" scenario — describe the dream morning/day WITH the product
- Show what it feels like, not what it does (features come later)
- Position as "a team member who thinks ahead" not "software"
- Zero pricing, zero features list — pure aspiration

**Email 5 — Social Proof & Results** (Day 7)
- Framework: Hormozi — Proof Stack; DK — Testimonials
- Lead with a specific result quote (real or clearly marked as example)
- Calculate the ROI in concrete terms (hours saved x rate = EUR saved)
- Second testimonial focusing on emotional relief
- Bridge: "They were skeptical too"

**Email 6 — Cost of Inaction** (Day 9)
- Framework: DK — Cost of Inaction; Hormozi — Opportunity Cost
- Full cost calculation:
  - Direct costs (admin time, missed opportunities, errors, liability)
  - Hidden costs (staff turnover, health, family, growth ceiling)
  - Total: "[amount]/year — and you're paying it right now, just not on an invoice"
- Hormozi quote: "The most expensive employee is you — when you're doing the wrong tasks"
- "Every week you wait, you pay the bill above"

**Email 7 — Fear Trigger with Solution** (Day 11)
- Framework: DK — Fear of Loss > Desire for Gain; Brunson — Epiphany Bridge
- Pick the scariest risk from research (liability, compliance, missed deadline)
- Make it vivid and personal
- Show how the product eliminates this risk specifically
- "This isn't nice-to-have. This is your shield."

**Email 8 — Deep-Dive on Pain #1** (Day 13)
- Framework: Hormozi — Remove Obstacles; DK — Specificity sells
- Take the #1 pain from research and go deep
- Vivid scenario with names, places, cascading chaos
- Show exactly how the product handles this scenario step by step
- End with the contrast: "Instead of [15 calls, 23 emails, 4 days] — one message."

**Email 9 — Deep-Dive on Pain #2** (Day 16)
- Framework: DK — Stack Value; Hormozi — Time to Value
- Take the #2 pain and calculate the time savings
- Before: [X hours per task × Y tasks per year = Z hours wasted]
- After: [fraction of the time]
- "What would you do with [Z] hours back?"

**Email 10 — Objection Handling** (Day 19)
- Framework: DK — Overcome Objections Before They're Raised; Brunson — Break external false belief
- Address the top objections head-on:
  - Data privacy / security
  - "I'm not a tech person"
  - "We've been burned by software before"
  - Integration with existing tools
- Be transparent and specific (certifications, guarantees, references)
- "Let your [compliance officer / data protection officer] talk to us. Before you start. Free."

**Email 11 — The Offer (Value Stack)** (Day 22)
- Framework: Hormozi — Grand Slam Offer + Value Stack; Brunson — Stack Slide; DK — The Close
- List 6-8 value components with individual values:
  - Component 1 (Value: EUR X/month)
  - Component 2 (Value: EUR X/month)
  - ...
  - Personal onboarding (Value: EUR X one-time)
- Total value: EUR [sum]/month + EUR [sum] setup
- Actual price: EUR [price]/month
- Risk reversal: "[X]-day pilot. If it doesn't deliver [specific result] — you pay nothing."
- Hormozi: "A good offer is so good that people feel stupid saying no."

**Email 12 — Last Chance & Future Pacing** (Day 25)
- Framework: DK — Urgency + Future Pacing; Brunson — Epiphany Bridge; Hormozi — FOMO
- "This is my last email."
- Two options laid out:
  - Option A: Continue as-is (list all the pains from research)
  - Option B: Start today (list all the benefits)
- Identity-level motivation: "You [serve others / build things / make impact]. Who takes care of you?"
- Scarcity: "[X] spots per month because every customer gets personal onboarding"
- P.S.: Shortcut to direct conversation

#### Email Writing Rules

**Structure:**
- **Subject line:** Curiosity-driven, specific, never clickbait. Use numbers or quotes when possible.
- **Opening:** No pleasantries. Drop straight into the pain or story. First line must hook.
- **Body:** 200-400 words. One core idea per email. Concrete scenarios with names, places, numbers.
- **Research integration:** Weave in the real quotes, stats, and scenarios found in Phase 0. The reader must think "this person understands my exact situation."
- **CTA:** Same CTA in every email (consistency builds familiarity).
- **P.S.:** Only in Email 1 and Email 12. Offer a shortcut (direct conversation).

**Tone:**
- Peer-to-peer, not vendor-to-buyer
- Empathetic but direct — no fluff
- Use the target group's own words and jargon (from research)
- Match formality to the audience (Sie vs. Du, formal vs. casual)

**Language rules (German):**
- NO "AI", "KI", "kuenstliche Intelligenz" in any email
- Use instead: "Automatisierung", "Entlastung", "Assistent", "uebernimmt fuer dich/Sie"
- Pain-first, solution-second in every email
- Industry jargon used correctly (from research)

**Critical rule:** No product pitch before Email 4. The first 3 emails are pure pain, empathy, and discrediting alternatives.

## Adaptation Notes

This playbook is venture-agnostic. When applying to a specific venture:
- Replace product/service references with the actual offering
- Adjust price points, guarantees, and scarcity to match the real offer
- Use real testimonials when available; mark placeholder testimonials clearly
- Adjust email length for the audience (C-suite reads shorter; practitioners read longer)
- For B2B SaaS: emphasize ROI math and integration ease
- For services: emphasize personal touch and relationship
- For nonprofits/NGOs: emphasize mission impact, not just efficiency

This combined playbook consumes Asset Foundation rows into both the landing page and the drip sequence:

- **Landing-page hero** ← Row 10 (Bodacious Claim) + Row 2 (Deepest Desires)
- **Landing-page pain story** ← Rows 3–5
- **Landing-page proof / stats** ← Row 11 (quantified)
- **Drip Email 1–3 (pain → false alternatives → cost of inaction)** ← Rows 3–5, Row 6, Row 8
- **Drip Email 4 (introduce the vehicle)** ← Row 9
- **Drip Email 11 (offer)** ← Row 10 + Row 11
